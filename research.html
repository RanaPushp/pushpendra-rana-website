Research

My research focuses on evaluating the real world impacts of environmental policies and climate interventions. I have studied topics such as forest certification, REDD+, tree planting programs, and climate finance initiatives across several countries. My broader interest is understanding how we can design climate, conservation, and forest management policies that are both scientifically credible and practically effective. That means combining insights from social science, ecology, and data science to better understand how complex social ecological systems behave and how interventions actually work in practice.

In the field of Responsible AI and sociotechnical system design, I aim to design and evaluate AI systems that remain robust under uncertainty, resistant to perverse incentives, and aligned with human values and governance constraints.

Causal integrity of climate policies and AI systems

My work on causal integrity of forest and carbon policies and AI systems focuses on ensuring that such policy and AI-driven decisions in forests, climate finance, and sustainability governance are grounded in credible causal reasoning rather than spurious correlations.

I develop frameworks that integrate causal inference, machine learning, and real-world experimentation to evaluate whether forest conservation, carbon policies, and AI systems generate reliable and actionable insights in complex social-ecological systems characterized by uncertainty, feedback loops, and interacting drivers. A central goal is to design AI-enabled learning systems that continuously update through human feedback, field data, and iterative evaluation, enabling adaptive decision-making rather than static optimization.

In domains such as forest restoration, carbon markets, and resource governance, this approach helps identify when policy interventions including AI tools and approaches improve outcomes, when they fail, and how they can be redesigned to align with institutional constraints, equity considerations, and long-term sustainability goals. By embedding causal integrity into policies and AI system design, my research aims to build decision-support systems that are not only predictive, but also trustworthy, contestable, and robust under real-world deployment conditions.

Responsible AI

My research identifies a key Responsible AI risk: ML-based studies can mistake randomness for causal structure in complex systems. Using causal machine learning methods — including DAGs, double machine learning, and interpretable ML — I have found that uncertainty in such estimations often exceeds the estimated effects, framing this not purely as a technical challenge but as a Responsible AI design challenge.

I am developing a general framework that computes feasibility bounds and risk envelopes before deploying any predictive or optimization model in real-world contexts, engineering AI systems to reflect uncertainty, avoid false precision, and preserve contestability — especially when outputs inform policy, finance, or resource allocation.

As a practical testbed, I co-founded WhereToPlant, an AI-driven tool that recommends tree species for planting in the Himalayas while reflecting uncertainty and fairness, and partnered with Humane Intelligence on a Bias Bounty challenge addressing transparency in AI-driven environmental decision-making.

Sociotechnical AI Design & Human–AI Interaction

My research frames ML as institutionally embedded decision support, requiring interpretability aligned with real human workflows. I explicitly integrate governance structures, incentives, and professional judgement into system design, revealing a persistent design mismatch between what institutions demand (project-level causality) and what AI/ML methods can estimate (averages) due to violations of causal assumptions in complex systems.

I am building a practical science for AI governance — interfaces and protocols that enable communities, frontline staff, and affected groups to contest AI outputs and improve the system. This work emphasizes trust, automation bias, override behavior, and the conditions under which human correction improves or degrades system performance.

Causal Evaluation of AI Systems

Using Randomized Control Trials, I causally isolate the effects of AI recommendations, disentangling them from institutional confounders and ecological heterogeneity. In an ongoing large-scale cluster RCT on forest restoration in the Indian Himalayas, I evaluate whether an AI-based decision-support system enables foresters and women's groups to iteratively improve outcomes through human-AI collaboration.

I aim to develop reusable evaluation protocols including open guidelines for field trials, reproducible causal estimators, and benchmark datasets for AI systems operating under real-world constraints.
